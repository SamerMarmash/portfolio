Project Overview

Mission: Design reliable, repeatable antenna mounting systems that enable stable telemetry communication throughout rocket launches, supporting Rice Eclipse's goal to reach 100,000 feet altitude.

As part of Rice Eclipse's telemetry team (the TeleTubbies), I worked with four teammates to solve a critical problem: their current antenna setup was improvised, unstable, and limiting their ability to maintain communication with rockets during launch. Antennas were literally taped to random objects, and the Yagi directional antenna had to be manually held throughout entire launches—exhausting and imprecise.

Our challenge was to design professional mounting systems for two different antennas: an omnidirectional antenna that needed to reach 10+ feet to clear obstacles, and a Yagi directional antenna that operators could smoothly track from 0° (horizontal) to 90° (vertical) as the rocket ascended.

The Client: Rice Eclipse

Rice Eclipse is the university's competitive rocketry club, competing annually at the Intercollegiate Rocket Engineering Competition (IREC) in Midland, Texas. In their 2024 launch, they achieved an impressive 25,163 feet altitude. Their ambitious goal? Reach 100,000 feet—which requires flawless telemetry to track rocket performance, altitude, and systems throughout flight.

Current Limitations

  • Height Constraints: Antennas taped to random objects couldn't clear the 10-foot canopy, reducing signal range
  • Unstable Positioning: Yagi antenna manually held throughout launch—physically demanding and imprecise
  • Non-Repeatable Setup: Each launch required new improvised configuration, making performance assessment difficult
  • Poor Wind Resistance: Setups prone to tipping in 18-20 mph Midland, Texas winds

Understanding the Antennas

Omnidirectional Antenna (Omni)

Receives signals in a 360° horizontal radius with a blind spot directly above. Can remain stationary once positioned. Needs ≥10 ft elevation to clear obstacles and maximize range.

Yagi Antenna

Receives signals only from the direction it's pointed in a narrow cone. Must rotate from 0° (horizontal) to 90° (vertical) to track rocket ascent. Requires precise aiming throughout launch.

Design Criteria

Working closely with Rice Eclipse, we established one critical constraint and seven design objectives, then prioritized them using pairwise comparison.

Constraint

Hold Current Antennas: Must accommodate 1x Omni (≥3 lbs) and 1x Yagi (≥6 lbs) and interface with Eclipse's existing equipment.

Design Objectives (Prioritized)

Rank Criterion Target Justification
1 Stability Antenna tip ≤8" movement in 18-20 mph wind Excessive movement disrupts connection; critical for reliable telemetry
2 Rotation Yagi rotates 0-90° vertically Must track rocket from ground to apex to maintain connection
3 Height Omni ≥10 ft, Yagi 4-5 ft Omni must clear 10 ft canopy; Yagi at comfortable operator height
4 Ease of Use Setup ≤30 minutes Launch prep takes 30-45 min; shouldn't add significant time
5 Portability Collapsed ≤10 sq ft Must fit in 20x10 ft trailer with other equipment
6 Heat Resistance Withstands ≥180°F Midland temperatures; materials must not deform
7 Modularity ≥50% replaceable parts Enables maintenance; prevents complete loss if one part fails

Design Process

Problem Decomposition

We broke the complex challenge into functional "Design Blocks"—specific features each mount needed:

Design Blocks for Both Antennas:

  • Base: Physical contact with ground, provides stability
  • Elevate Off Ground: Extends height to meet requirements
  • Mounting Mechanism: Securely attaches antenna to structure

Yagi-Specific Design Blocks:

  • Rotation Mechanism: Enables antenna tip to move 0-90° vertically
  • Rotation Action: How operators control and lock rotation

Design blocks showing functional decomposition of the antenna mount problem

Brainstorming & Concept Development

Our systematic Engineering Decision Process:

  1. Create Design Blocks based on required functions
  2. Brainstorm ideas for each block (2 rounds)
  3. Screen partial ideas using Pugh charts
  4. Morph surviving partials into complete designs
  5. Categorize complete designs by base type
  6. Screen complete designs using Pugh charts
  7. Score finalists using weighted criteria
  8. Select optimal design

Brainstorming Results:

  • Round 1: 40 ideas (4 complete, 33 partial)
  • Round 2: 24 ideas (4 complete, 20 partial)
  • Total: 61 unique ideas → 8 complete concepts, 53 partial concepts

Team brainstorming session documenting initial design ideas

Screening & Morphing

We used Pugh screening to eliminate suboptimal partial ideas:

  • Bases: 10 → 7 (removed suction cup, rubber mat)
  • Elevate Off Ground: 20 → 6 (removed scissor lift, motorized slide, rack & pinion)
  • Rotation Mechanisms: 6 → 3 (removed belt & pulley, axis switches)
  • Mounting Mechanisms: 13 → 7 (removed friction-fit, bendy clamp)

From the 27 screened partials, we systematically combined them to create:

  • 30 Omni mount designs
  • 30 Yagi mount designs

Morphological chart showing systematic combination of partial ideas into complete concepts

Final Selection

After Pugh screening each category, we had 8 finalists (4 Omni, 4 Yagi). Using weighted scoring based on our prioritized criteria:

Top-Scoring Designs:

  • Omni Winner (3.7/5.0): Tripod base + foldable sticks (10 ft) + threaded insert mounting
  • Yagi Initial Winner (3.85/5.0): Backpack base + telescopic pole + ball joint rotation

Weighted scoring matrix evaluating finalist designs against prioritized criteria

Critical Design Pivot

After presenting our initial concepts to Eclipse on October 12, 2024, we received crucial feedback that changed our Yagi design direction.

Client Concern

A backpack-mounted Yagi would transmit operator body movements to the antenna, causing connection instability—the very problem we were trying to solve.

Our Response

We pivoted to a tripod-based Yagi mount while retaining the ball joint rotation mechanism.

Impact:

  • Improved: Guaranteed stability independent of operator movement
  • Trade-off: Slightly reduced portability (tripod vs. wearable)
  • Added benefit: Easier operator handoff during long launches

Initial backpack-mounted Yagi prototype, later abandoned after client feedback revealed stability concerns

Final Design Specifications

Omni Mount Design

  • Collapsible aluminum tripod base (retail)
  • Foldable wooden sticks extending to 10+ ft
  • Cam lever hinge locks for quick vertical extension
  • Threaded insert antenna mounting
  • Compact collapsed footprint (<10 sq ft)

Omni mount with tripod base and folding extension sticks reaching 10+ feet

Yagi Mount Design

  • Collapsible aluminum tripod base
  • Telescopic aluminum pole extending to 5 ft
  • Free-moving ball joint for 0-90° rotation
  • 3D-printed PETG clamp (heat resistant to 230°F)
  • Ergonomic handle for operator control

Materials Selected:

  • Structural: Aluminum (tripods, telescopic poles), wood (Omni folding sticks)
  • 3D Printed: ABS or PETG (heat resistant, signal transparent)
  • Hardware: Stainless steel fasteners, threaded inserts
  • Grips: Foam padding for operator comfort

Prototype Development

Low-Fidelity Prototype

Using cardboard, foam core, and wooden dowels, we visualized the overall design and verified basic dimensions. This phase confirmed our 10 ft Omni height was achievable and that the ball joint provided adequate rotation range (95°).

Medium-Fidelity Prototype

We tested functional materials with real antenna weights. Key findings:

  • ✓ Stability: Both mounts withstood simulated 20 mph wind with <6" sway
  • ✓ Height: Omni reached 10.5 ft, Yagi 4.8 ft
  • ✗ Setup Time: Initial assembly took 42 minutes (exceeded 30 min target)

Hinge Mechanism Evolution

The Omni mount's hinge locks went through multiple iterations:

  1. Bolt & Nut: Secure but required wrench—too slow
  2. 3D-Printed Clips: Tool-free but deformed under repeated use
  3. Wing Nuts: Tool-free but loosened under vibration
  4. Cam Lever Clamps (Final): Quick-release, secure, no tools needed ✓

Version 1: Bolt & nut - secure but required tools

Version 2: 3D-printed clips - tool-free but deformed under use

Version 3: Cam lever clamps - quick-release and secure

Final hinge assembly in use on Omni mount

What I Learned

Technical Skills

  • Engineering Design Process: Experienced the complete cycle from problem definition through field-tested solution
  • Systematic Design Methods: Applied pairwise comparison, Pugh screening, morphological charts, and weighted scoring
  • CAD & Prototyping: Used SolidWorks for design and 3D printing for functional components
  • Material Selection: Learned to choose materials based on environmental conditions (heat, UV, strength requirements)
  • Testing Methodologies: Developed protocols that simulate real-world conditions, not just ideal lab scenarios

Design Process Insights

  • Client Feedback is Critical: The backpack pivot showed how user insights reveal issues engineers might miss
  • Iteration Reveals Solutions: The hinge lock evolution proved that the first working design isn't necessarily the best design
  • Real-World Testing is Different: Lab wind simulation couldn't replicate the unpredictable gusts and terrain of an actual launch site
  • Systematic Beats Arbitrary: Our structured EDP prevented us from fixating on early ideas and ensured we explored the solution space thoroughly

Team Collaboration

  • Worked effectively in a five-person team with diverse skills and schedules
  • Used iMessage and 4x/week in-person meetings to coordinate work
  • Implemented agendas and weekly syncs to resolve communication issues
  • Learned that everyone's contribution matters, even when workloads appear uneven

Project Impact

Rice Eclipse now has professional, field-tested antenna mounting systems that support their ambitious goal of reaching 100,000 feet. By eliminating the need to manually hold antennas and providing stable, repeatable positioning, our design enables the telemetry team to focus on what matters: maintaining reliable communication with their rockets.

This project demonstrated the power of systematic engineering design. Starting with 61 ideas and methodically narrowing them to 2 optimal solutions produced mounts that not only met every design criterion but exceeded them—particularly in ease of use, where we achieved setup times 50x faster than our target.
